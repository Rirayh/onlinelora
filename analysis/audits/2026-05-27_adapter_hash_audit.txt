## Interpretation

Adapters are NOT bit-identical and NOT cuDNN-equivalent. They genuinely
differ — but the differences come from:

1. **`best_step` varies**: dr=0.30 saved at step 250, others at step 500.
   Saving at different optimization steps produces genuinely different LoRA
   states.
2. **Saliency calibration RNG drift**: `saliency_calib_n` differs across
   runs (v2_full: 64, v1_recheck: 256, tie-break: 256). Saliency calib
   consumes RNG state at startup → diverges per-step train state.
3. **Code-drift between commits**: different `commit_hash` per run
   (v2_full: 547f8c9, v1_recheck: bf5d452, tie-break: 87da7d4). Bug fixes
   between commits change exact training trajectory.

## PI hypothesis verdict

PI's claim "all adapters are bit-identical pre-merge LoRA snapshots" =
**REFINED, not falsified**:

- ✅ TRUE: every saved adapter IS the pre-merge state (`best_step ∈ {250,
  500}` < first merge at step 750).
- ✅ TRUE: drop policy NEVER affects the saved state (only fires at step
  ≥750, never observed by best/).
- ❌ But adapters are NOT bit-identical because of (1)-(3) above.

So the adapters differ measurably, but **none of those differences encode
the drop method's effect**. The lm-eval scores produced by these adapters
DO vary, but the variance reflects best-step / RNG / code-drift, not
method behavior. The scoreboard "ranking" was meaningless.
